About Me

My love of food was kindled from a young age. I fondly recall hours spent watching my parents cook in the kitchen and yearning for an early taste of what would be served for dinner that day. However, my fascination with cooking was ignited in high school, when I worked as a sushi chef part-time in a local Japanese-American fusion restaurant. From stumbling into the restaurant at 7AM on Saturdays to prepare 50 lb of sushi rice to spending the next 7 hours actually preparing the sushi, the experience not only taught me perseverence and discipline, but also cultivated my passion. I would spend the next year and a half honing my skills and learning how to cook a variety of other Asian fusion dishes.

Now in college, I no longer enjoy the luxury of a having an industrial kitchen at my disposal, but still enjoy cooking and baking in my free time. For me, it serves as not only a way to practice and further develop my skills, but also as a pastime to enjoy with friends. I occassionally post on my food instagram and intend to use this page as an avenue to post recipes that I've had much success with in the past as well as commentary on what I found to be useful while following them.

Food Related Experiments

Dry Aging Meat

Over the summer of 2018, I tried dry-aging my own NY striploin in a secondhand mini-fridge I purchased for $30. After cleaning the fridge, I cut the $110 striploin (purchased from a local butchery) in half and wrapped it in cheesecloth (to help prevent too much initial moisture loss). I set up some racks in the fridge as well as a thermometer (to keep the temperature between 35-40F) and a fan (to keep constant air circulation). I dry-aged them for 60 days and got some pretty neat results, check them out!

Recipes

Matcha Macarons

I've made macarons plenty of times before, but they've never come out as good as they did with this recipe. The keys I've found are to avoid overmixing (just until you can make a full figure 8 using the mixture without it breaking) and to let it rest for at least half an hour after tapping the air out of them. At around 15 minutes in the oven, you should start watching the macarons, I took them out at 17 minutes for a softer texture and better colour. As for colouring, always use food gel colouring and make sure you put more than the actual colour you want because it lightens up in the oven.

Egg Tarts

A classic when it comes to Chinese desserts. I didn't have enough when I was younger, so I always fill up on them whenever I come across a Chinese bakery or when I make them at home! I ended up using two recipes, one for the crust (which I found to give a super flakey and amazing crust) and one for the filling. For the crust, make sure you don't let it rest too long overnight (will ruin the air pockets and make it not flakey) and avoid opening the oven too often when baking. For better colour on the crust, I found that egg-washing it and then baking for an extra couple of minutes worked well.

Crème Brûlée

A simple yet delicious dessert that all of my friends claim is their "favourite." The instructions in the linked page are very concise and easy to follow. If you're too impatient to wait for the crème brûlées to cool, you can put them in the freezer for 20-30 minutes. Additionally, a mixture of white sugar and dried brown sugar on top was amazing for colour and taste.

Steamed Fish

This is a super simple but delicious dish. Make sure you buy a fresh whole fish for this (and then have the butcher clean and scale it for you). Some tips for choosing good fish is to make sure that its eyes are clear (not cloudy) and that it doesn't have an overwhelmingly fishy smell. If the smells makes you nauseous, then it's most likely not fresh. Also, make sure you have a wok/steamer that's big enough to steam this fish in!

Ramen

This was an incredibly humbling experience since it took ~2 days to make the broth and chashu from scratch and the taste was nowhere near that of some of the better ramen places I've been to. In retrospect, I would've added much umami into it (maybe anchovies, some more MSG or pork fat?). Make sure you keep simmering the bones to get as much broth as possible out of them (even drain it and then fill it with water for a second batch).
